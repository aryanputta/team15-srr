1.5.4.1   CDH Subsystem Overview

The command and data handling (CDH) subsystem provides the command authority, onboard autonomy, telemetry management, science-data storage, and communications interface for the mission. It receives and validates commands from Earth, executes onboard sequences, time-tags the dust, temperature, luminescence, and radiation measurements that make up the primary science return, manages onboard storage of that data, generates telemetry, and routes data to the communications path for return to Earth or to a lunar relay orbiter (NASA 2016). The subsystem hosts the flight software that maintains spacecraft state and preserves command execution through cruise, descent, surface checkout, and permanently shadowed region (PSR) operations, while supporting low-power safe mode and autonomous fault response when real-time command is unavailable (NASA 2009).

Because the lander retains a direct line of sight to Earth at the landing site, near 83.21 degrees south and 203.775 degrees east, while the rover loses that line of sight inside the PSR, the governing design drivers are radiation-driven upset risk in the avionics, intermittent communications, and the need to protect buffered data until a contact window opens. Figure 1. CDH command and data flow. Commands enter via CCSDS / Proximity-1, are validated before execution, and science data is buffered under a priority policy and downlinked within the 48-hour target.
Technology Readiness

Hardware subassemblies rate TRL 6 to 7 on small-spacecraft and CubeSat-class lunar heritage; the mission-specific flight software is TRL 5 because its integrated fault-management and autonomy configuration is not yet validated in a relevant environment. The overall subsystem TRL is set by the least mature element, fixing it at TRL 5 (NASA 2018).

1.5.4.2   CDH Subsystem Requirements

The requirements below are written as measurable shall statements, each traced to a parent mission requirement from Section 1.4 and assigned a verification method. Parents are the survival requirement SYS-1, the mass requirement SYS-2, the communication requirements CH-1 and CH-1.1, the energy requirements SYS-4 and ELEC-1, and the science requirements SCI-1, SCI-2, and SCI-3. The traceability is shown in Figure 2. The radiation requirement CDH-05 is engineering-derived, since the mission requirements specify no total ionizing dose; its 30 krad (Si) threshold carries roughly six times margin over the 3.6 to 4.8 krad expected at the site (Phipps et al. 2024). The downlink target of CDH-06 is an engineering-derived 48 hours, verified by the mission latency simulation.

1.5.4.3   CDH Subsystem Trade Studies

Each architecture decision is scored against the Section 1.6 evaluation criteria using the percentage method, with weights summing to 100 percent and grades on a 0/1/5/10 scale. The communications architecture trade is decisive because the rover loses Earth line of sight in the PSR (CH-1.1). The relay-via-orbiter option is selected; the mission latency simulation confirms that direct-to-Earth meets the 48-hour target for only 59 percent of acquisitions while relay meets it for all of them (Figure 4).

Figure 3a. Trade A — Communications architecture. Relay-via-orbiter selected (79.00%).
Figure 3b. Trade B — Flight computer. Radiation-tolerant SBC + EDAC selected (90.00%); meets the derived 30 krad requirement with margin while the COTS part fails radiation.

1.5.4.4   Communications Subsystem

The communications subsystem provides the command uplink, telemetry downlink, and science-data return. Because the rover operates inside a PSR with poor or no direct Earth line of sight, the link routes through a lunar relay orbiter across short scheduled windows over the 29.5-day mission. The design must close the relay link with margin under worst-case geometry, use CCSDS and Proximity-1 protocols, and survive 70 K to 395 K, radiation, dust, and landing shock (CCSDS 2020; CCSDS 2013). The rover carries a body-fixed medium-gain antenna to avoid a dust- and cold-vulnerable pointing mechanism, while the lander retains a steerable element for its direct-to-Earth path.

1.5.7.3   CDH Subsystem Redundancy

The flight computer is a block-redundant pair with a cold spare energized after a watchdog-triggered failover, because it is a single point of failure and the 29.5-day intermittent-contact mission allows no ground-commanded swap (NASA 2009). Storage applies error detection and correction and duplicates the highest-priority products until downlink is confirmed. The communications path is functionally redundant through the lander direct-to-Earth path and the relay path, and the internal data bus carries redundant routing on critical command and telemetry lines. On a confirmed critical fault the flight software enters safe mode, preserves state across the reset, records evidence, and fails over to the cold spare, returning the fault log at the next contact window.
